Tracking renewables tripling and fossil fuels phaseout

In 2023, at COP28, governments agreed to triple global renewable capacity by 2030.

Tripling renewables capacity to at least 11,000 GW by 2030 keeps a pathway for limiting global warming to 1.5C within reach, according to independent analyses from the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA) and the International Energy Agency (IEA).

Tripling renewables is possibly the most powerful action the world can take within the next decade to transition away from fossil fuels, accounting for up to two-thirds of emissions reductions by 2050.

Global Energy Monitor’s tracking of energy facilities across the sector provides a detailed view of progress toward this critical goal.

More than 5,500 utility-scale projects are set to come online before 2030, totaling 1,240 GW — equal to about 70% of the capacity additions needed for tripling.

Another 20,000+ projects are in development without a confirmed start date, totaling 3,854 GW.

If all are completed in time, the world could meet the tripling target. But delays, cancellations, and competing demand for power mean that outcome is far from certain.

To stay on track, the renewable pipeline must keep expanding — and projects already underway must be delivered on time.

Small-scale renewable projects — mostly distributed solar — will also contribute to the total. While distributed solar will play a crucial role, it cannot fully compensate for shortfalls in utility-scale development.

Tripling renewables globally does not mean every country will triple its own capacity. Countries started from very different levels in the 2022 base year, so the scale of change required varies.

Countries with a large existing renewables base will see smaller relative increases. Brazil, for example, already has substantial utility-scale hydropower. Countries starting from a smaller base, meanwhile, could multiply capacity several times over by 2030 — not necessarily because of especially high ambition, but simply because they are starting from a lower level.

Still, independent assessments show that increases of tripling, or greater, are needed for a 1.5C-aligned pathway, especially in the largest economies. These countries would need to scale renewables by roughly 2.5 to 3.1 times by 2030.

So far, some of the richest economies are falling short. Even if all in-development renewable capacity in the G7 and EU were completed by 2030, total capacity would increase by only 1.8 times in the G7 and 2.4 times in the EU.
